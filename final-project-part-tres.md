## Changes Made Since Part II

After receiving feedback on Part II, I decided to focus on the main turning points of my story and create three data visualizations to support the narrative. For my first data visualization, I presented it to friends for feedback and made sure to include the replacement rate in the visualization, along with a brief blurb explaining what it is, since I was running into people who had no idea what it meant. Below, the graph I submitted for part II, followed by my final graph.

For my second visualization, I wanted to show the decline and the gap between the 2018 and 2024 data. Initially, I made a dumbbell visualization showing this. However, every time I presented it, I had to explain what I was trying to say, so I ended up taking it out of my final presentation and replacing it with a side-by-side bar graph, which was easier for people to understand. Below is my dumbbell graph, followed by my final visualization. 

For my final visualization, I wanted to show the gap between rural and urban births. In Part I and Part II, I proposed showing this through a graph of Colombia. However, I knew that my classmates wouldn’t be able to identify which areas of the map represented rural and urban areas, which defeated the purpose of quickly communicating my data. I made a 100% stacked bar chart. Initially, this bar only had the rural births data because that was my main focus. However, during my in-class presentation, some students seemed confused about the percentages and suggested I incorporate the urban data back in. ## Audience 

My audience is the same as the one in Part II. My target audience is Colombians, especially young adults, journalists, and policymakers who are following the fertility debate and need data to move beyond panic headlines towards a broader understanding of the issue. However, I also added information and made changes, keeping in mind that I was going to present this project to a mostly American audience during class.
